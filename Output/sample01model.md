## 0) Original draft (verbatim from source)

Below is the original draft text from `Data/ACE/ACE sample 1.md`, with **annotation and explanation placed immediately after each paragraph**.

### Are chatbots suitable companions for young people?

### Paragraph 1 (Original)
In a world connected by social media, many young people still feel very isolated and struggle to form real offline relationships (Twenge, 2019). Chatbots, designed to mimic human conversations and offer companionship, have received mixed reactions (Kretzschmar et al., 2019) (Context). While chatbots may seem like helpful companions, they are not suitable for young people because they intensify feelings of loneliness and create a false sense of reality, failing to provide true companionship (Thesis Statement).

**Annotation:** [CTX] + [ARG/Thesis]  
**Explanation:** This paragraph performs the introduction function: it establishes relevance (youth loneliness), narrows to chatbots, and ends with a clear thesis that previews two reasons (loneliness + false reality). This strongly supports rubric criteria for thesis clarity and argument structure.

### Paragraph 2 (Original)
Despite their human-like interactions, chatbots tend to increase loneliness in young people rather than reduce it (Claim). According to Pani et al. (2024), chatbots provide quick satisfaction but only partly meet social needs, which require face-to-face interaction to be fully satisfied. Their ease of use can trick young people into thinking real relationships are not essential, discouraging them from actively building social skills and networks vital for healthy growth (Turkle, 2017). For example, social chatbots such as Replika AI, meant to offer companionship, can lead to addiction and social withdrawal because they easily form strong bonds with users (Pani et al., 2024) (Evidence/Grounds). This implies that users who tend to gravitate toward such chatbots are likely to engage in excessive use that prioritises human-machine interactions over real-world social engagement, intensifying feelings of isolation (Explanation/Warrant).

**Annotation:** [C] + [G] + [W]  
**Explanation:** This is a full Toulmin mini-cycle. The claim is explicit, evidence is source-based, and the warrant explains the mechanism (easy AI interaction displaces real social practice). Strong grounding and reasoning align with Critical Thinking & Use of Grounds.

### Paragraph 3 (Original)
Some might argue the ready availability and human-like features of chatbots, such as their ability to showcase empathy, make them ideal companions for combating loneliness, especially among marginalised youth who lack emotional support (Counterargument of Rebuttal). However, this argument overlooks the potential of a reinforcing loop. For example, a teenager suffering from social anxiety may turn to a chatbot for comfort since AI never judges people. Over time, the teenager will withdraw from real-world interactions when relying solely on the chatbot for emotional support. As the in-person social skills weaken from disuse, face-to-face interactions become even more intimidating, trapping the teenager in a cycle of avoidance, ultimately deepening the teenager’s sense of alienation and loneliness (Response of Rebuttal).

**Annotation:** [CA] + [R]  
**Explanation:** The writer acknowledges a plausible opposing view, then rebuts it by identifying a hidden assumption (“safe use naturally helps”) and replacing it with a causal loop (avoidance cycle). This matches Module 3 rebuttal strategies and improves dialectical depth.

### Paragraph 4 (Original)
Chatbots give young people a false sense of reality by being ambiguous about their limitations (Claim). Most chatbots do not explicitly acknowledge their inability to reciprocate emotions with helpful replies or equivalent emotional depth, since they do not possess any lived experiences or feelings. Chatbots’ failure to adequately respond to young people’s personal struggles can leave them feeling dismissed or unsupported, and through portraying a false reality, hinder them from seeking help in the real world (Zhang et al., 2025). Compared to mental health professionals, chatbots are not sensitive enough to subtlety and understatement, and can underestimate the importance and urgency of users’ queries (Wester et al., 2024) (Evidence/Grounds). The unawareness that chatbots lack reciprocity may lead young people, who are in a critical phase of developing social and emotional intelligence, to form unrealistic expectations of a genuine companionship (Explanation/Warrant).

**Annotation:** [C] + [G] + [W]  
**Explanation:** This second claim follows the same strong structure as Paragraph 2: clear claim, multiple grounds, and a warrant that links emotional-development risk to unrealistic expectations. The parallel structure improves coherence and rubric-level logical interconnectedness.

### Paragraph 5 (Original)
Some argue that chatbots do not deceive users but rather provide a safe, simulated environment for young people to practise social interactions and build confidence to carry over into their real-world relationships (Counterargument of Rebuttal). It is worth pointing out that this view assumes that users will naturally outgrow chatbot dependence. Unfortunately, in reality, many young people, once accustomed to the simplicity of AI interactions, may avoid the complexities of human relationships altogether. Chatbots may replace real relationships, encouraging users to avoid human interaction and misunderstand true social connection (Response of Rebuttal).

**Annotation:** [CA] + [R]  
**Explanation:** Again, the rebuttal is assumption-focused. Instead of denying all benefits, the response challenges transferability and dependence assumptions, which is a stronger rebuttal style than simple contradiction.

### Paragraph 6 (Original)
Despite demonstrating some resemblances to human-like conversations, chatbots can only at best simulate superficial features of supportive relationships or promote short-term improvements in mood. Chatbots exacerbate loneliness by replacing genuine human connections with empty interaction and fostering the false belief that emotional reciprocity with AI is possible (Summary). With the right intentions, chatbots can be useful assistive tools for young people, but in no way should they assume the role of a companion or as a substitute for genuine human interactions (Final remark).

**Annotation:** [SUM] + [Final Position]  
**Explanation:** The conclusion synthesizes both claims and adds a nuanced boundary condition (assistive tool vs. companion replacement). This prevents overgeneralization and strengthens academic tone.

## 1) Big-picture takeaway: what this essay does well

This sample is a strong **textual mentor** because it consistently does the following:

1. Establishes context quickly and ends the introduction with a **clear thesis stance**.
2. Uses **two claims** (aligned with ACE Final expectations in Module 3 adaptation of Toulmin).
3. Supports each claim with **grounds (evidence)** + **warrant (explanation)**.
4. Includes a **counterargument + response** for each claim.
5. Closes with a concise synthesis and practical limitation statement (chatbots as assistive tools, not companions).

In rubric language (`Data/ACE/rubrics.md`), this sample tends toward high performance in:
- **Critical Thinking & Use of Grounds (30%)**
- **Argument Structure (30%)**
- **Academic Tone & Clarity (30%)**
- **Citations & References (10%)**

## 2) Overall framework you can reuse for writing this essay type

Use this reusable 7-move framework for ACE:

### Intro paragraph (Move I1–I3)
- **I1 Context**: Frame social problem + relevance.
- **I2 Topic narrowing**: define focus (e.g., chatbot companionship).
- **I3 Thesis (Argument + reason map)**: clear stance + preview of two claims.

### Body paragraph 1 (Move B1.1–B1.5)
- **B1.1 Claim 1 topic sentence**
- **B1.2 Grounds (evidence with citation)**
- **B1.3 Warrant (why evidence supports claim)**
- **B1.4 Counterargument**
- **B1.5 Response/rebuttal**

### Body paragraph 2 (Move B2.1–B2.5)
- Repeat the same logic with Claim 2.

### Conclusion (Move C1–C2)
- **C1 Synthesis of two claims**
- **C2 Final remark / limitation / policy implication**

> Quick memory aid from Module 3 “sturdy house” activity (2.8):  
> **Claim = roof**, **Grounds = foundation**, **Warrant = walls**, **Counterargument = storm**, **Response = storm shutters**.

## 5) How this sample aligns with the ACE rubric

### A) Critical Thinking & Use of Grounds (30%)
- Strength: Two rebuttal cycles (CA + response) show actual dialectical thinking.
- Strength: Multiple scholarly sources used as grounds.
- Improvement opportunity (for students): In some places, add slightly more precise mechanism language (e.g., *how exactly* dependence forms over time).

### B) Argument Structure (30%)
- Strength: Components are mostly present and connected.
- Strength: 2-claim architecture mirrors ACE Final expectations in Module 3 adaptation.
- Improvement opportunity: Ensure each response directly targets the specific assumption in the counterargument (not just general disagreement).

### C) Academic Tone & Clarity (30%)
- Strength: Formal lexical choices, clear topic sentences, coherent paragraph progression.
- Improvement opportunity: Remove small redundancies (e.g., repeated “young people”) through stylistic variation.

### D) Citations & References (10%)
- Strength: Frequent in-text citation usage and reference list provided.
- Improvement opportunity: For direct quotations (if used), maintain strict APA punctuation and page requirements.

## 6) Mentor lessons you can transfer to your own ACE task

1. **Write thesis as a two-lane roadmap.**  
   Example pattern: “X is not suitable because A and B.”
2. **One claim = one mini-argument cycle** (C → G → W → CA → R).
3. **Don’t let evidence “float.”** Always explain why it supports your claim.
4. **Counterargument should be strong, not strawman.**
5. **Response should target a hidden assumption.**  
   (Module 3 Activity 2.6 “Exposing Logical Flaws” and Activity 2.7 “Giving a Response”).
6. **Conclusion = synthesis + boundary condition.**  
   (e.g., “useful as a tool, not as replacement”).

## 8) Exercises on organizing logical flow (with answer keys)

## Exercise 1: Paragraph order puzzle
Reorder these functions into ideal ACE order:
- (a) Counterargument + response
- (b) Context + thesis
- (c) Claim + grounds + warrant
- (d) Conclusion

**Answer:** (b) → (c) → (a) → (d)

## Exercise 2: Sentence flow repair
Given sequence:
1) Evidence appears.  
2) Claim appears.  
3) Warrant appears.

Reorder for strongest flow.

**Answer:** Claim → Evidence → Warrant (C → G → W)

## Exercise 3: Rebuttal precision check
Counterargument: “Chatbots help anxious teens practise conversations safely.”

Choose the best response:
- A. “That is wrong because chatbots are bad.”
- B. “This assumes practice transfers automatically; however, low-stakes scripted exchange may reduce tolerance for ambiguity in real interpersonal situations, limiting transfer.”

**Answer:** B (targets assumption + explains mechanism)

## Exercise 4: Warrant quality upgrade
Claim: “Chatbots increase loneliness.”  
Grounds: “Users interact 30% less with humans.”

Write a one-sentence warrant linking them.

**Sample answer:** “Because frequent social practice builds relational confidence, reduced human interaction narrows opportunities to maintain those skills, which can intensify social disconnection over time.”

## Exercise 5: Rubric-based self-check (quick scoring)
Rate your paragraph 0–2 on each:
- Thesis clarity
- Claim-ground-warrant connection
- Counterargument quality
- Response relevance
- Academic tone

**Interpretation:** 8–10 = strong draft, 5–7 = revise logic, <5 = rebuild structure first.

In recent years, [topic context]. Although [opposing surface appeal], [your thesis stance] because [claim 1 summary] and [claim 2 summary].

### Body Paragraph 1
[Claim 1 topic sentence]. According to [source], [grounds]. This suggests that [warrant: why evidence supports claim]. While some argue [counterargument], this overlooks [assumption flaw]. In reality, [response + mechanism].

### Body Paragraph 2
[Claim 2 topic sentence]. [Grounds with citation]. [Warrant]. Some may contend [counterargument]. However, [response that neutralizes it].

### Conclusion
Overall, [synthesis of claims]. Therefore, [restated stance], while [nuanced boundary condition / practical implication].
