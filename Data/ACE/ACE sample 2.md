### Is it advisable for schools to adopt facial recognition technologies on campus?

Facial recognition technology (FRT) is becoming more common in schools worldwide, with institutions in Australia, the United States, and China using it for tasks like tracking attendance, improving campus security, and monitoring student behaviour (Andrejevic & Selwyn, 2020; Selwyn et al., 2023). FRT, which identifies people by analysing their facial features, is marketed as a way to simplify school operations by automating routine tasks (Nidhya et al., 2024). However, its use is highly debated. While supporters claim that FRT reduces teacher workload and increases efficiency with tools like AutoRoll, an educational FRT introduced in Australia, reportedly saving teachers 2.5 hours per week by automating roll calls (Selwyn et al., 2023), the technology also brings significant challenges. Despite its promised efficiencies, the implementation of FRT in schools introduces significant technical, legal, and ethical complications that ultimately create more administrative burdens than it resolves.

FRT increases administrative workload by introducing complex technical demands that divert resources from education. While marketed as a tool to streamline operations, FRT requires schools to manage ongoing troubleshooting, software updates, and dedicated staff training (Selwyn et al., 2023). For instance, systems like AutoRoll, which shifted to localised data processing to address privacy (Selwyn et al., 2023), require schools to handle hardware synchronisation and new IT support logistics, creating unforeseen challenges. These hidden technical burdens consume significant time and financial resources, forcing administrators to oversee technology rather than support students, thereby contradicting the core promise of reducing the workload. Those touting the strengths of newer systems, on the other hand, counter that their design prioritises user-friendliness, allowing educators to manage attendance with minimal technical knowledge through simplified interfaces and automated

processes (Nidhya et al., 2024). They argue that these intuitive features are specifically engineered to eliminate, not create administrative tasks. However, this argument overlooks the substantial and ongoing administrative effort required to correct the system's errors. Studies show that biased algorithms can misclassify individuals, especially those with darker skin, at error rates as high as 34.7% (Buolamwini & Gebru, 2018), necessitating frequent manual intervention by staff to fix misidentifications and conduct bias audits, which pulls resources away from educational priorities and adds to administrative burdens.

Beyond its technical flaws, FRT imposes significant legal and ethical burdens on schools by enforcing biased behavioural norms that stigmatise non-neurotypical students. The technology's propensity for error is not random; it is often systematic, built on algorithms trained to recognise "engagement" or "attention" based on narrow, neurotypical standards, such as sustained eye contact and specific facial cues (Andrejevic & Selwyn, 2020). This can lead to discriminatory outcomes where students with autism, ADHD, or other learning differences are consistently misclassified as inattentive or disruptive, forcing schools to manage the fallout from unfair punishments and potential violations of inclusivity policies (Selwyn et al., 2023). Ethically, schools become complicit in a system that pathologises normal neurological diversity, damaging trust and creating extra work to rectify its harms.

Some might counter that these issues of algorithmic bias and error are only temporary technical obstacles. They argue that as the technology advances, biases can be addressed on a case-by-case basis through software updates and improved training data, allowing schools to reap the benefits of increased security and efficiency without sacrificing the promise of objective monitoring. However, this argument is flawed because the core problem is not just a technical glitch but a fundamental misconception of human behaviour. As Bala (2020) illustrates, FRT assumes behaviours like attention deficits and lack of eye contact are under conscious control and are inherently negative, which is not true. The technology cannot distinguish between intentional inattention and the unintentional mind-wandering that is a normal cognitive function and is even linked to greater creativity and working memory. Most critically, it pathologises the natural expressions of non-neurotypical children, potentially mislabelling their valid learning methods as "negative behaviours”. This means that schools will face a constant administrative burden, having to constantly correct its errors and manage the complaints and legal challenges that result from its use.

While facial recognition technology promises operational efficiencies for schools, its current adoption in schools creates more problems than it solves. The administrative burden introduced by its technical complexity and the relentless legal and ethical oversight it demands ultimately divert crucial resources away from education. Until FRT can fundamentally address its flaws in accuracy, bias, and data ethics, schools should prioritise simpler, more reliable solutions that enhance rather than complicate their primary educational mission.
