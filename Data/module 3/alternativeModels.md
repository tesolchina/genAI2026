Apart from the Toulmin Model, there are a number of other argumentation models. Two of the more well-established models are the Rogerian argument and the Classical argument.  
The Rogerian Argument 
Originated by the American psychologist Carl Rogers, the Rogerian argument is a conflict- resolution approach in rhetoric that focuses on reaching a common ground between opposing perspectives. By validating each side’s valid points, it fosters compromise and collaborative problem-solving rather than engaging in a debate. Unlike the Toulmin model, there is no uniform structure of the Rogerian argument.

The Classical Argument 
The Classical argument (or Aristotelian argument) is a foundational model of persuasion developed by Aristotle, the ancient Greek philosopher and pioneer of rhetorical theory. Rooted in his seminal work Rhetoric, this structured approach aims not merely to present an opinion, but to systematically convince an audience through logic (logos), credibility (ethos), and emotional resonance (pathos).
