What are Backing & Qualifier? 
Since Backing and Qualifier are sometimes absent in an argument, both components are not required in the Argument Construction and Evaluation (ACE) Draft and Final assignments. However, you may still learn how they help further strengthen your argument.  
Backing is the provision of broader, credible and relevant evidence (e.g., facts, research studies, expert opinions) to support the warrant used to connect the grounds and the claim.  
Example: “Democratic societies prioritise individual rights and freedoms, including the right to privacy and freedom from unwarranted surveillance. These principles are enshrined in international human rights declarations and many national constitutions.” 
Qualifier intends to limit the claim’s scope through:  
• Adverbs, e.g., likely (probability), often, sometimes (frequency)  
• Subordinating conjunctions, e.g., unless, if, as long as, on condition that (condition)  
• Prepositions, e.g., except, except for, without (condition)  
Example: “Facial recognition technologies threaten individuals’ rights to freedom and privacy, unless stringent safeguards and regulations are put in place to prevent misuse and protect individual rights.” 

Developing Your Rebuttal  
While Backing strengthens the Warrant by providing a broader source of credible support, and the Qualifier indicates the precise strength and scope of the claim, these components are not always necessary—especially in topics with limited evidence available for forming well- supported arguments. For now, let us focus on mastering rebuttals. We will focus on Backing and Qualifier in Part 3 of this module when we learn how to strengthen our arguments further. 
A rebuttal consists of two parts:  
• Counterargument (a viewpoint that challenges the claim of the opposing argument) 
• Response (the ’counter’ to the counterargument, attempting to neutralise its threat to 
the original claim or challenge its validity) 
Example: 
Component Example 
1. Argument “Chatbots cannot be a true friend to young people.” 
2. Claim “Chatbots are unable to provide critical feedback or challenge users.” 
3. Evidence (Grounds):  "Chatbots never judge, never disagree, always say ‘I understand’.” 
4. Explanation (Warrant):    A true friend will be honest and tell you when you are wrong.  
5. Rebuttal (Counterargument) Users have the ability to request that chatbots challenge them, allowing for critical feedback when desired.  
(Response) However, this approach is not equivalent to the organic and spontaneous pushback provided by a true friend, as it relies on user initiation rather than the natural, intuitive honesty that characterises genuine human relationships. 

 Below are some guiding questions for developing a strong rebuttal: 
• Under what conditions might the claim not be valid/true? 
• What assumptions does the claim rely on? Are they always accurate or applicable? 
• Are there exceptions to the claim that challenge its general validity? 
• How might someone with a different perspective or experience disagree with the 
claim? 
• What specific evidence or example might someone use to argue against the claim? 
• If a counterargument weakens the claim, how can you respond to it by refining, 
limiting, or providing additional support for your original position?
