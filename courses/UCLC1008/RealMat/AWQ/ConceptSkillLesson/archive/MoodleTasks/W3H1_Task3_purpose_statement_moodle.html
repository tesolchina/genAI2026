Week 3 Hour 1 — Task 3: Purpose statement

Module 1 · Activity 2.4 · Rubric: Academic Tone & Clarity (understanding the paper’s central claim)

1. Reading materials

What is a purpose statement?

The purpose statement is the central argument or main point of the entire paper. It is usually found toward the end of the Introduction. It should be a debatable claim (not a mere fact or question) that the rest of the paper supports.

Article A — Purpose and framework (from Abstract & Discussion)

From the abstract: “To address this gap in the literature, this study examined how different degrees of technological innovativeness and dangerous beliefs in the virtual world (DBVW) influence parents' perceived value of using and intention to continue supporting schools' use of face recognition systems.”

From the discussion: “In line with this, the present study explored parents' DBVW and technological innovativeness in the value perception of the use of face-recognition systems, and continuous intention to use such systems as a research framework.”

Article B — Abstract (claim and focus)

“While critical commentators are beginning to question the pedagogical limitations of facially driven learning, this article contends that school-based facial recognition presents a number of other social challenges and concerns that merit specific attention. This includes the likelihood of facial recognition technology altering the nature of schools and schooling along divisive, authoritarian and oppressive lines. Against this background, the article considers whether or not a valid case can ever be made for allowing this form of technology in schools.”

Not purpose statements (for comparison)

These are data, topic sentences, or evidence, not the overall purpose:

  • “The average score of parents' perceived value is 3.919.” → data
  • “Another application of FRT in schools is attendance monitoring.” → topic sentence
  • “Facial data lends itself to constant surveillance.” → evidence

2. What to do

Write one sentence that could serve as Article B’s purpose statement: a debatable claim + the article’s focus (e.g. social challenges and concerns about FRT in schools; whether a valid case can be made for FRT).

3. How to reply (Moodle forum)

Your post

  1. Click Reply in this forum discussion.
  2. Write one sentence that could act as Article B’s purpose statement (claim + focus).
  3. Make sure it is a debatable claim, not just a fact or a question.
  4. Post your reply.

Example: “Andrejevic and Selwyn (2020) argue that school-based facial recognition raises serious social challenges and concerns, and the article considers whether a valid case can ever be made for allowing FRT in schools.”

Reply to one peer

  1. Read a classmate’s post.
  2. Click Reply to their post.
  3. Write: Is it debatable? Answer Yes or No, and give one brief reason.

Example: “Yes. It states a clear claim about FRT’s challenges and poses a debatable question about whether FRT in schools can be justified.”

4. Teacher: verbal prompts

Multiple-choice (optional, ~1 min)

Which is a purpose statement rather than a topic sentence?

  • A) “The average score of parents' perceived value is 3.919.”
  • B) “This study examined how technological innovativeness and DBVW influence parents' perceived value and continuance intention.”
  • C) “Another application of FRT in schools is attendance monitoring.”
  • D) “Facial data lends itself to constant surveillance.”

Pair discussion (~1 min)

Ask students to work in pairs and say aloud:

  • “What is the main claim of Article B?”
  • “Where would you expect to find the purpose statement in an article (e.g. Introduction, end of Intro)?”

Tip: Use these prompts before or after students post on the forum, as fits your class flow.
