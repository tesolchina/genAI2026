Module 2 · Part 1 · Rubric: Paraphrasing (own words; no copying)

1. Reading materials

Strategies for paraphrasing

  • Synonyms — e.g. “recognition” → “detection”; “option” → “choice”
  • Word form — e.g. “affect” (verb) → “effect” (noun) → “effectively” (adverb)
  • Voice — active ↔ passive (e.g. “The researcher reviewed the literature” ↔ “The literature was reviewed by the researcher”)

Use 2–3 strategies together when paraphrasing. Change both words and sentence structure; keep the meaning accurate; always cite the source.

Example — “Too close” vs “Better”

Source (Article B): “There is no option for students to self-curate and restrict what data they ‘share’.”

Too close (avoid): “There is no option for students to self-curate and restrict what data they share (Andrejevic & Selwyn, 2020).”

Better (own words + structure + citation): “Andrejevic and Selwyn (2020) argue that students cannot choose what facial data they share.”

Sentence to paraphrase (Article A)

“The average score of parents’ perceived value is 3.919, which is much higher than the neutral level (3.000).”

You may generalise the numbers (e.g. “well above the neutral level”) when paraphrasing.

4. Teacher: verbal prompts

Multiple-choice (optional, ~1 min)

Which is the best paraphrase of “parents were willing to try technological innovation”?

  • A) Parents were willing to try technological innovation.
  • B) Parents were willing to try tech innovation.
  • C) Parents showed openness to adopting new technology (Hong et al., 2022).
  • D) Parents liked technology (Hong et al., 2022).

Pair discussion (~1 min)

Ask students to work in pairs and say aloud:

  • “Which option would you choose, and why?”
  • “Paraphrase aloud: ‘facial data lends itself to constant surveillance.’”

Tip: Use these prompts before or after students post on the forum, as fits your class flow.
