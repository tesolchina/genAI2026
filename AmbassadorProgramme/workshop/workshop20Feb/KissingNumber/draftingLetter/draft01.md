# Draft 01 — SCMP letter (kissing number / PackingStar)

I refer to your report on the Chinese team’s use of AI to advance the “kissing number” problem (“Romance between scientists and AI,” 19 February).

The kissing number asks how many identical balls can touch one central ball without overlapping—a question that has intrigued mathematicians since Newton. In many dimensions the answer is still unknown. The team’s AI system, PackingStar, has achieved record-breaking results: new lower bounds in several dimensions and the first major progress in rational constructions in 13 dimensions since 1971. That is a genuine scientific advance, and it is worth celebrating.

This story also highlights the importance of human input at every stage of AI-driven research. The problem itself was posed by generations of mathematicians; the team had to reproduce known results before claiming new ones. A key design idea—a second “player” that corrects flawed choices—came from watching a mathematician manually remove bad entries. Verification and interpretation of what the AI finds still rest with people, and mathematicians have already built on the AI’s patterns to improve bounds further. As the team put it, it is “human and machine exploring the universe together.” When we use AI for research, including in maths, we should keep humans in the loop: posing questions, designing methods, and checking meaning.
