The kissing number: how many identical spheres can touch a central sphere of the same size without the spheres overlapping? Photo: Handout
Victoria Bela
Published: 2:00pm, 19 Feb 2026
Chinese scientists have used artificial intelligence to make progress on a more than 300-year-old maths problem that has implications for large-scale data storage and advanced telecommunications.
Using an AI system called PackingStar, the researchers made record-breaking advances on the “kissing number” problem, surpassing the limits of human geometric intuition and standard computing.

The work done by the team was like a “romance” between machines and humans exploring science together, they said in a video posted by Peking University on Saturday.

“These results showcase AI’s power to understand high-dimensional geometry, reshape established mathematical intuitions, and drive progress on century-old geometry problems,” the team said in a paper first published in the open-access online research repository arXiv in November.

The preprint paper – meaning it has not undergone peer review – was written by researchers from Peking University, Fudan University, and the Shanghai Academy of AI for Science (SAIS).


Is AI better at maths than mathematicians?
English polymath Isaac Newton’s kissing number problem first emerged in a debate with Scottish mathematician David Gregory in 1694.
It led to the question of how many identical spheres could be arranged to simultaneously touch, or kiss, a central sphere of the same size, without the spheres overlapping.

One of the authors of the Chinese paper, Qi Yuan – a chief scientist at SAIS and a professor at Fudan University – said that in three dimensions, the problem could be imagined like a pomegranate packed with seeds.

“If you replace the seeds with spheres, how many unit spheres can touch the central one?” Qi said in the video posted by Peking University.

Newton believed that the solution to the kissing number problem in three dimensions – the one our everyday, observable world exists in – was 12 kissing spheres, while Gregory believed 13 were possible. It was definitively proven in 1953 that the answer was 12.

In 1900, mathematician David Hilbert included the sphere packing problem in the 18th of his famous set of 23 problems that have guided research in maths.

Though the kissing number problem sounds simple, it connects the fields of geometry, information theory and number theory, and has far-reaching implications.

“In information coding, you want to compress the most information using the fewest bits, and that’s closely related to the sphere packing problem,” Qi said.

In engineering, the optimal arrangement of spheres is similar to the optimal distribution of communications signals, which plays a role in quantum coding and satellite communication, according to a report by China Science Daily on Sunday.

The answer to the problem has been proven in dimensions 1, 2, 3, 4, 8 and 24. Mathematician Oleg Musin proved in 2003 that the answer in the fourth dimension was 24, while the answer in the 24th dimension – 196,560 kissing spheres – was proven nearly 50 years ago.

In other dimensions, mathematical and technological advances have allowed for lower and upper bounds of the kissing number to be determined, but definitive solutions have yet to be found.

“As the dimension increases, human understanding becomes more limited due to the growing geometric complexity of high-dimensional spaces,” the Chinese team said in the paper.

With the help of AI, the researchers achieved breakthroughs in the kissing number problem, notably discovering new lower bounds in dimensions 25 to 31, areas where progress had previously stalled.

They created a reinforcement learning system called PackingStar, which framed the problem as a cooperative game between two AI agents to explore high-dimensional spaces too complex for standard computers.

“Trained from scratch without any human-designed priors, PackingStar automatically discovers high-dimensional kissing configurations that outperform all known constructions and exhibit clear mathematical structure,” the paper said.

The team was able to construct sphere arrangements in dimension 13 that marked the first major progress in rational structures for this dimension since 1971. They also found thousands of new sphere arrangements that could help to expand understanding of sphere packing.

However, the researchers noted that PackingStar could not provide mathematical proof for the new configurations so humans would be required to verify the results.
