# Partner AI writing: process and transferable lessons

**Context.** I (Simon Wang) wrote an SCMP letter with AI assistance: *Tax companies that deploy AI, save Hong Kong graduates* (published 6 Feb 2026). The collaborative process is documented in a Poe conversation: https://poe.com/s/JdBL1zQr2xd9jamabP16. Published letter: https://www.scmp.com/opinion/letters/article/3342445/tax-companies-deploy-ai-save-hong-kong-graduates

---

## Process (as inferred from the published letter and typical partner-AI workflows)

1. **Anchor in a concrete news hook**  
   The letter explicitly refers to a recent SCMP article (“55% plunge in new hires…”, 2 Feb). The AI partnership works best when the human supplies the trigger (headline, date, link) so the draft stays timely and on-topic.

2. **Human provides stance and expertise**  
   The author brings identity (“university educator”) and a clear angle: structural change driven by AI, need for curriculum reform, and policy (tax + ESG). The AI helps structure and sharpen the argument, not invent the position.

3. **Iterative drafting**  
   Typical flow: human gives bullet points or a rough draft → AI tightens wording, fits length (e.g. SCMP’s ~400-word limit), improves flow → human edits for voice and facts. The published letter is concise, with short paragraphs and a clear call to action.

4. **Fact-check and ownership**  
   The human remains responsible for accuracy (e.g. “55% plunge”, ESG framing). AI can suggest phrasing; the author verifies numbers and policy claims before submission.

5. **Matching outlet conventions**  
   SCMP Letters have a specific style: “I refer to…”, clear thesis, evidence, then policy/recommendation. The process benefits from giving the AI an example (e.g. another SCMP letter) or a short style note so the output matches the format.

---

## Transferable lessons

- **Use a single, shareable thread**  
  Keeping one Poe (or similar) conversation for a letter makes it easy to revisit, refine, and later document “how this was written.”

- **Lead with the hook and your view**  
  Start by pasting the article (or summary) and 2–3 sentences on what you want to say. That keeps the AI from drifting and keeps the letter in your voice.

- **Constrain length and structure early**  
  Tell the AI the word limit and preferred shape (e.g. “refer to article → problem → policy ask → closing”). Reduces back-and-forth later.

- **One main ask**  
  The tax/graduates letter centres on one policy idea (tax AI deployment to fund junior positions) plus an ESG angle. One clear ask is easier for readers and editors than many scattered points.

- **Reuse the same workflow for new letters**  
  For the kissing-number letter: same steps—anchor in the SCMP report, state your angle (e.g. what this means for the public, for AI–human collaboration), draft with AI, then fact-check and edit. The Poe link serves as a template for “partner AI writing” you can cite or share.

- **Acknowledge AI assistance if you wish**  
  Depending on outlet policy and your preference, you can mention in a cover note or privately that the letter was drafted with AI assistance; the published byline stays the author’s.

- **Hook:** SCMP report “Romance between scientists and AI…” (19 Feb 2026) and the PackingStar/kissing number preprint.
- **Your role:** Provide the take (e.g. what the public should notice, why it matters beyond maths). No need for advanced math—focus on collaboration, intuition, and implications.
- **AI’s role:** Help turn bullet points into a short, clear letter that fits SCMP style and length, and suggest accessible analogies (e.g. “like two players improving a shared design” for the two-agent system).
- **Output:** A draft in `work/` that you edit, fact-check, and submit; and optionally an updated note here on what you changed after the AI draft.
