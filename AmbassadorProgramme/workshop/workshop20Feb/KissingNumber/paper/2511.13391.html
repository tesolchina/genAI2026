Finding Kissing Numbers with Game-theoretic Reinforcement Learning

Abstract

Since Isaac Newton first studied the Kissing Number Problem in 1694, determining the maximal number of non-overlapping spheres around a central sphere has remained a fundamental challenge. This problem is the local analogue of Hilbert’s 18th problem, bridging geometry, number theory, and information theory. Although significant progress has been made through lattices and codes, the irregularities of high-dimensional geometry, dimensional structure variability, and combinatorial explosion beyond Go limit the scalability and generality of existing methods. Here we model the problem as a two-player matrix completion game and train the reinforcement learning system, PackingStar, to play the games. The matrix entries represent pairwise cosines of sphere center vectors. One player fills entries while another corrects suboptimal ones to improve exploration quality, cooperatively maximizing the matrix size, corresponding to the kissing number. These matrices are decomposed into representative substructures, providing diverse bases and structural constraints that steer subsequent games and make extremely large spaces tractable. PackingStar surpasses records from dimensions 25 to 31 and sets new lower bounds for generalized kissing numbers under various angular constraints. It achieves the first breakthrough beyond rational structures from 1971 in 13 dimensions and discovers over 6000 new structures in other dimensions. Notably, some configurations challenge long-held antipodal paradigms, revealing algebraic correspondences with finite simple groups as well as geometric relationships across dimensions. Inspired by these patterns, humans devised further improved constructions. These results demonstrate AI’s power to explore high-dimensional spaces beyond human intuition via extreme-scale reinforcement learning and open new pathways for the Kissing Number Problem and broader geometry research.

Introduction

The Kissing Number Problem was first posed in 1694 by Isaac Newton and David Gregory, asking for the maximal number of non-overlapping spheres that can touch a central sphere in three-dimensional space. It was later extended to nn-dimensional Euclidean space, where K​(n)K(n) denotes the kissing number in nn dimensions, sparking centuries of research in high-dimensional geometry. In 1900, David Hilbert included the sphere packing problem in nn-dimensional space as the 18th problem of his famous 23 problems [?]. The Kissing Number Problem is the local analogue of Hilbert’s 18th problem [?]. It connects to geometry, number theory, group theory, and information theory [?,?,?]. Therefore, advancing this problem could foster progress across multiple fields.

Unfortunately, determining kissing numbers in higher dimensions remains exceptionally difficult. Table 1 lists the kissing numbers in different dimensions. After centuries of effort, the problem has been solved exactly only in a few dimensions 1, 2, 3, 4, 8, and 24 [?,?,?,?,?,?,?]. As the dimension increases, human understanding becomes more limited due to the growing geometric complexity of high-dimensional spaces and combinatorial explosion of configurations. Over the past half-century, mathematicians have continued to push the boundaries using lattice constructions [?], code techniques [?], group representation theory [?], and carefully designed substructures derived from known configurations [?]. In dimensions 10, 11, 13, 14, and dimensions from 17 to 21, such approaches established new lower bounds [?,?]. In dimensions 25–31, studies conducted in 2011 and 2016 further improved the bounds by exploiting subsets of Leech lattice vectors combined with heuristic optimization [?,?]. While these results mark significant milestones, they are limited by reliance on prior knowledge, and specific human design. As dimensionality increases, the exponentially growing configuration space and the breakdown of full symmetry assumptions make existing approaches insufficient. These challenges highlight the need for novel paradigms to explore complex structures beyond human intuition.

In this paper, we introduce PackingStar, a multi-agent reinforcement learning system that reformulates the Kissing Number Problem as a two-player matrix completion game in the Gram matrix domain. By operating directly on pairwise cosines rather than high-dimensional coordinates, this formulation eliminates numerical instability and computational inefficiency, enabling fully parallelizable GPU acceleration and large-scale sampling. Within PackingStar, the first agent incrementally fills matrix entries to construct larger and denser configurations. In early exploration stages, however, the candidate space is exponentially large and only local matrix information is observable, making erroneous placements inevitable. To address this challenge, the second agent performs delayed and selective corrections by leveraging a more global understanding of matrix, dynamically removing invalid or inconsistent entries and compressing the first agent’s search space. This interaction enables the joint system to converge toward larger configurations corresponding to higher kissing numbers. Meanwhile, the evolving matrices are iteratively decomposed into representative substructures that distill essential geometric patterns and impose structural constraints for subsequent games. Together, these mechanisms transform unconstrained combinatorial exploration into guided construction, substantially reducing reinforcement learning complexity in exponentially large spaces and enabling scalable discovery of high-dimensional configurations previously inaccessible to existing methods.

PackingStar demonstrates remarkable scalability, successfully reproducing all known kissing number configurations in dimensions up to 31. Figure 1 illustrates some of PackingStar’s breakthrough discoveries on this problem. From dimensions 25 to 31, PackingStar surpasses the lower bounds reported in 2016 and also exceeds the construction form introduced in 2011 [?,?], achieving what appears to be the optimal construction form along this path; the resulting construction form are summarized in Table 2. Notably, the breakthrough configuration discovered in 25 dimensions exhibits a clear geometric pattern that corresponds closely to the substructure of Leech lattice. Across multiple metrics, this configuration shows strong convergence, suggesting that it may represent the optimal 25-dimensional arrangement, although a rigorous mathematical proof is still lacking. In 13 dimensions, PackingStar surpasses the previously known rational structure with 1130 spheres from 1971 [?], reaching a kissing number of 1146. Rational structures are those in which all pairwise cosines between sphere centers are rational. The non-rational structure with 1154 spheres is obtained by slightly adjusting the coordinates of some spheres in the 1130-sphere configuration, later reached 1154 in 1999 [?]. Although the new 13-dimensional rational configuration does not exceed the non-rational record, it is of considerable mathematical value. Its fully rational inter-sphere cosines allow for exact analytic study without relying on numerical approximations. This offers insights into the effects of different constraints on kissing numbers and may inspire higher-density packings. Rational structures also facilitate more efficient encoding and decoding in information and communication applications, highlighting their practical significance alongside their theoretical and geometric value. Beyond these specific dimensions, PackingStar uncovers over 6000 new configurations in 14 dimensions and identifies additional configurations corresponding to current lower bounds in dimensions 12, 13, 15, and others. The sheer volume of these newly discovered arrangements provides rich opportunities for further investigation and may ultimately lead to configurations with even higher kissing numbers. In addition, PackingStar achieves significant new records for generalized kissing configurations under tighter angular constraints, including 12-dimensional, 20-dimensional and 21-dimensional configurations with 81, 405 and 567 spheres under the 1/41/4 cosine constraint, as well as 14-dimensional and 17-dimensional configurations with 252 and 578 spheres under the 1/31/3 constraint, respectively. Notably, several of the newly discovered configurations fundamentally challenge long-held antipodal paradigms. Rather than conforming to symmetry-driven constructions centered around antipodal pairing, these configurations exhibit asymmetric yet highly regular geometric patterns. In multiple cases, they reveal deep algebraic correspondences with finite simple groups and previously unnoticed geometric relationships across dimensions. These structural insights have, in turn, inspired mathematicians to extract new and more general construction principles. Building on patterns uncovered by PackingStar, subsequent human-designed configurations have further improved existing lower bounds in several dimensions, including 22 dimensions, illustrating a productive feedback loop between AI-driven discovery and mathematical theory. All of these results have been independently validated and are now included in the standard databases for kissing numbers and spherical codes. In summary, these results showcase AI’s power to understand high-dimensional geometry, reshape established mathematical intuitions, and drive progress on century-old geometry problems.

Results

We evaluate the PackingStar system across multiple high-dimensional regimes and report new state-of-the-art kissing configurations. Our results fall into three categories: (i) new lower bounds in dimensions 2525–3131, (ii) improved rational constructions in 1313 dimensions, (iii) diverse families of solutions in different dimensions, and (iv) new records for generalized kissing configurations. These findings demonstrate that PackingStar can both recover classical lattice-based structures and uncover entirely new high-dimensional arrangements, substantially expanding the landscape of known kissing configurations. The main kissing configurations are available at the data repository https://github.com/CDM1619/PackingStar.

New records for generalized kissing numbers

In this section, we apply PackingStar to explore generalized kissing configurations with maximal inner product at most 1/31/3 and 1/41/4. For geometric intuition, these cases may be viewed as double-sphere and triple-sphere kissing configurations, in which one asks how many additional unit spheres can be simultaneously tangent to two or three mutually tangent spheres, respectively. These configurations arise naturally as fundamental building blocks in sphere packings and play an important role in the study of the broader packing problem.

The classical kissing number is denoted by K​(n)K(n), which corresponds to the case in which the pairwise angles between sphere centers have cosine at most 1/21/2. More generally, for an angular constraint −1<α<1-1<\alpha<1 with α≠1/2\alpha\neq 1/2, we use K​(n,α)K(n,\alpha) to denote maximum number of unit vectors such that the inner product between any two distinct vectors is at most α\alpha.

As shown in Table 3, the new constructions from PackingStar yield new record for K​(n,α)K(n,\alpha) in several dimensions. In 12, 20 and 21 dimensions under the 1/41/4 constraint, we obtain Knew​(12,1/4)=81K_{\mathrm{new}}(12,1/4)=81, Knew​(20,1/4)=405K_{\mathrm{new}}(20,1/4)=405 and Knew​(21,1/4)=567K_{\mathrm{new}}(21,1/4)=567, surpassing the previous records of 79, 378 and 554, respectively. We also report improvements under the 1/31/3 constraint: in dimensions 14 and 17, we obtain Knew​(14,1/3)=252K_{\mathrm{new}}(14,1/3)=252 and Knew​(17,1/3)=578K_{\mathrm{new}}(17,1/3)=578, which beat the prior best known records of 240 and 546.

Notably, the Knew​(12,1/4)K_{\mathrm{new}}(12,1/4) configuration contains no antipodal pairs; however, each of the 81 spheres has the same number of neighbors at each distance, forming a highly regular local structure. Such a combination of high local regularity without any antipodal pair appears to be rare in spherical code. We find several distinct variants of Knew​(12,1/4)K_{\mathrm{new}}(12,1/4) configuration, among which the most symmetric realization turns out to be the Kronecker product of the Schläfli spherical code with the unit equilateral triangle. This arrangement possesses a transitive symmetry group of order 311040. In dimension 17, PackingStar found a significant improvement: a configuration with 578 spheres, exceeding the previously known 546. All three geometrically non-isomorphic variants discovered share the same cosine set. Analysis of these variants reveals a remarkable structural property: the Knew​(17,1/3)K_{\mathrm{new}}(17,1/3) configuration can be interpreted as essentially two copies of the 16-dimensional 288-sphere configuration, which is known to be optimal under the cosine 1/41/4 constraint, together with two additional points, i.e., 578=288×2+2578=288\times 2+2. This decomposition provides strong evidence that 578 is very likely optimal for the cosine 1/31/3 constraint in dimension 17.

Methods

In this section, we present a learning-based system PackingStar to find high-dimensional kissing constructions. First, we extract dominant geometric features of candidate kissing configurations via simulation and Monte Carlo Tree Search. Second, we formulate the search for maximal kissing numbers as a cooperative two-player matrix completion game, where one agent iteratively fills the Gram matrix and the other corrects suboptimal entries, jointly maximizing the final matrix size. This formulation allows all operations to be parallelized at large scale on NVIDIA GPUs using low-level mathematical operators in cuBLAS library, fully exploiting computational power to accelerate both the game simulation and model training.

Finding Kissing Numbers in two-player matrix completion game

Let 𝐆(m0)∈ℝm0×m0\mathbf{G}^{(m_{0})}\in\mathbb{R}^{m_{0}\times m_{0}} be the initial partial Gram matrix of the sphere center vectors. We formulate the matrix completion process as a two-player sequential game, corresponding to Step 2 and Step 3 in Figure 2. Each player is modeled as a learning agent, and the two agents are jointly optimized through game-theoretic reinforcement learning to cooperatively search for larger kissing numbers. This alternating filling-correcting process over NN rounds can be formulated as a Markov game, where the matrix 𝐆(m)\mathbf{G}^{(m)} represents the state, Player 1’s action is the sampled entry 𝐠\mathbf{g} that extends the matrix, Player 2’s action is the selected index set II for correcting entries, and the shared team reward is the number of rows of the final matrix.

Game Dynamics and Cooperative Learning Process.

The policy π2\pi_{2} is learned to identify and remove suboptimal entries filled by Player 1, so that in subsequent filling steps, Player 1 can expand the matrix further. The alternating filling-correcting process continues until no further feasible entries exist for Player 1, and Player 2 cannot correct suboptimal entries. where the expectation accounts for the stochasticity of both policies, and 𝐆(M)​({𝐠},I)\mathbf{G}^{(M)}(\{\mathbf{g}\},I) denotes the matrix obtained after all filling and correcting steps under policies π1\pi_{1} and π2\pi_{2}. In short, the policy of Player 1 is implemented as a tree-search learner, where node values are updated based on the team reward obtained after each episode. The policy of Player 2 is parameterized by a neural network and optimized via policy gradient. After each round, both the tree search statistics of Player 1 and the policy parameters of Player 2 are jointly updated under the same team reward, enabling the two players to mutually improve and construct larger kissing configurations. Matrix Decompositionn and Initialization for Subsequent Games.

Once a final completed matrix 𝐆(M)\mathbf{G}^{(M)} is obtained, it is iteratively decomposed into a collection of submatrices

𝐆J,J(M),J⊂{1,2,…,M},\mathbf{G}^{(M)}_{J,J},\quad J\subset\{1,2,\dots,M\}, (16)

where each index set JJ selects a subset of rows and columns from 𝐆(M)\mathbf{G}^{(M)}. These submatrices capture representative geometric patterns and serve as distilled structural bases. For subsequent filling games, each submatrix 𝐆J,J(M)\mathbf{G}^{(M)}_{J,J} is used as an initialization matrix, providing structured guidance and constraints for Player 1 while exploring new entries. Multiple such submatrices can be used to initialize parallel filling games, allowing the system to explore diverse regions of the high-dimensional search space simultaneously. Formally, for each submatrix 𝐆∗=𝐆J,J(M)\mathbf{G}^{*}=\mathbf{G}^{(M)}_{J,J}, Player 1 resumes filling according to policy π1\pi_{1}, and Player 2 applies corrections via π2\pi_{2}, continuing the alternating process.

Conclusion

Trained from scratch without any human-designed priors, PackingStar automatically discovers high-dimensional kissing configurations that outperform all known constructions and exhibit clear mathematical structure. The key idea is to reformulate the Kissing Number Problem as a two-player matrix completion game over the Gram-matrix domain, enabling efficient exploration of an extremely large and sparse space without explicit coordinate representations. Notably, PackingStar improves all known lower bounds from dimensions 25 to 31 and sets new records for generalized kissing configurations under various angular constraints. A current limitation is that PackingStar cannot provide optimality proofs for these new configurations. We anticipate that the interaction between reinforcement learning and rigorous proof techniques will not only open new pathways for the Kissing Number Problem, but also extend to broader geometry problems.

Appendix A Key difference to AlphaEvolve

The key difference between PackingStar and AlphaEvolve lies in the search space. AlphaEvolve optimizes explicit coordinates in ℝn\mathbb{R}^{n}, which is numerically unstable and scales exponentially with dimension; increasing computational resources cannot overcome this fundamental limitation. In contrast, PackingStar operates in the Gram-matrix domain with strict geometric constraints, combined with iterative decomposition into representative substructures and cooperative game dynamics that compress and distill high-dimensional search spaces. These techniques fundamentally render exploration of extremely large and complex spaces tractable, enabling efficient large-scale sampling. This fundamental distinction explains why PackingStar scales to higher dimensions while AlphaEvolve cannot [?].

Although AlphaEvolve improved the kissing number in 11 dimensions from 592 to 593, likely by adjusting the coordinates of the known 592-sphere configuration, the result lacks clear geometric structure. Furthermore, AlphaEvolve relies on a specific 11-dimensional structure with 592 spheres, which is not jammed, making it difficult to transfer similar techniques to structures in other dimensions. In contrast, PackingStar not only sets new bounds across higher dimensions but also discovers geometrically meaningful, analyzable configurations, providing concrete objects for further mathematical study.

In summary, AlphaEvolve demonstrates that AI can “optimize” geometry, PackingStar shows that AI can truly “create” geometry. This represents the first genuinely systematic breakthrough of AI in high-dimensional discrete geometry and coding theory: it is not merely a numerical improvement, but a methodological advance, illustrating how AI can uncover new structures and insights in previously unexplored high-dimensional spaces.
