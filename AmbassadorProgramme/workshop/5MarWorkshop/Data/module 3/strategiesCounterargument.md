There are a couple of strategies that can be used to formulate counterarguments. Below are some of the examples. The argument proposed by the opposite side that you are going to challenge is highlighted in green while the suggested counterarguments are highlighted in blue. 
Strategy 1: Concession + Counterargument (The “Yes, But…” Approach) 
Template:  
"While there is some truth in the idea/belief that [argument proposed by the opposite side], this overlooks/fails to account for [key flaw/limitation]. In reality, [your response] because [evidence/logic]." 
Example: “While there is some truth in the idea that social media helps people stay connected, this overlooks the rise in 'empty' interactions. In reality, passive scrolling harms mental health because Twenge (2019) found heavy users report 50% higher loneliness."

Strategy 2: Evidence-Based Counterargument 
Template:  
"Proponents argue [argument proposed by the opposite side], but [authority/study] casts serious doubt on this claim/calls this claim into question. Their research shows [contradictory evidence], undermining the view/claim that [paraphrase of opposing claim]" 
Example: "Proponents argue chatbots reduce loneliness, but Stanford’s 2024 study casts serious doubt on this claim. Their research shows chatbot users interact 30% less with humans, thereby potentially worsening isolation, undermining the claim that loneliness can be alleviated by chatbots."

Template:  
"This argument assumes [flawed premise proposed by the opposite side], but it ignores [critical factor]. For example, [specific counterexample], which seriously undermines the claim.” 
Example: "This argument assumes more online friends will result in less loneliness, but it ignores the role of the quality of interactions. For example, a 2023 Nature study found Instagram users with 1,000+ followers felt lonelier than those with 10 close friends, which seriously undermines the claim."

Strategy 4: Alternative Solutions 
Template:  
"Instead of [solution proposed by the opposite side], we could [better alternative], which achieves [shared goal] without [negative consequence].” 
Example: "Instead of relying on AI chatbots, we could fund school counselling programmes, which provide real emotional support without discouraging human connection.”

Strategy 5: Questioning Relevance or Scope 
Template:  
"The claim that [argument proposed by the opposite side] is irrelevant/misleading because [context difference]. Unlike [their example], [your topic] involves [key distinction].” 
Example: "The claim that social media connects billions is irrelevant because quantity does not guarantee quality. Unlike face-to-face friendships, online interactions involve a lack of emotional depth (Turkle, 2017).”

Strategy 6: Ethical or Value-Based Counterargument 
Template:  
"Even if [argument proposed by the opposite side], it is unethical to [harmful action] because [core value]. For instance, [example of harm].” 
Example: "Even if chatbots are convenient, it is unethical to replace human care with simulations because vulnerable teens deserve authentic support. For instance, AI cannot perform crisis intervention like a psychologist can.”
